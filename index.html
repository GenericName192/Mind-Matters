Mental Health Made Simple

Common Issues (Hover each one over for help!)

Depression

Persistent sadness, low energy, or loss of interest in things you used to enjoy. It can affect your sleep, appetite, and motivation.

Do one small task—like getting out of bed or making a cup of tea. Small steps help build momentum.

Anxiety

Constant worry, nervousness, or fear—sometimes without a clear reason. It may come with physical symptoms like a racing heart or trouble sleeping.

Use the 5-4-3-2-1 grounding technique to calm your mind and stay present.

Feeling Alone

Feeling like no one understands you or that you have no one to talk to, even if you're around people.

Message someone you trust or say hello to someone nearby—small connections make a big difference.

Stress and Burnout

Feeling overwhelmed, mentally exhausted, or unable to cope with day-to-day pressures—especially from work, school, or responsibilities.

Break big tasks into smaller steps. Focus on just one thing at a time.

Panic attacks

Sudden waves of intense fear or discomfort, often with symptoms like shortness of breath, chest pain, or dizziness.

Breathe slowly: in for 4, hold for 4, out for 6. It helps calm your body and mind.

Low Self-Esteem

Thinking badly about yourself, struggling with confidence, or constantly feeling “not good enough.”

Speak to yourself like you would to a friend—with kindness, not criticism.

Managing Stress

1. Take Regular Breaks

It's easy to get caught up in school, work, or daily tasks without giving yourself time to pause. Taking short, regular breaks helps you reset both mentally and physically. Even a quick walk, stretching, or stepping outside for fresh air can lower stress levels and boost focus. Breaks aren't a waste of time—they're an important part of looking after yourself.

2. Keep a Simple Routine

Having a basic daily routine can bring structure and comfort to your day, especially when things feel overwhelming. It doesn’t need to be strict—just a simple plan that includes time for meals, rest, and activities you enjoy. Routines help your mind feel more in control, reducing the unpredictability that often feeds stress.

3. Practice Deep Breathing

Stress can make your body feel tense and your thoughts race. Deep breathing is a simple but powerful tool to help you calm down. Try this: inhale slowly through your nose for four seconds, hold for four, then exhale gently through your mouth for six. Doing this for just a few minutes can help slow your heart rate and clear your mind.

4. Talk to Someone You Trust

You don’t have to carry stress alone. Talking to someone—a friend, family member, teacher, or support service—can help you feel lighter and more supported. Sometimes just saying how you feel out loud can bring relief. Opening up takes courage, but it's a strong step toward feeling better.

5. Get Enough Sleep

Sleep plays a huge role in how we handle stress. When you’re well-rested, you’re more able to cope with daily challenges. Try creating a calming bedtime routine, like turning off screens an hour before bed, dimming the lights, or listening to calming music. Aim for a consistent sleep schedule, and don’t feel guilty for making rest a priority—it’s essential for your wellbeing.
